Gold Making Charges in Nepal 2026 — What You Pay & How to Negotiate

Understanding udhyog kharch (making charges) before you buy gold jewellery in Nepal can save you thousands of rupees.

Guide
📅 July 20, 2026✍️ goldpricenepal.online⏱ 6 min read

When you buy gold jewellery in Nepal, the price you pay is not just the gold value — it also includes making charges (known locally as udhyog kharch or banaune kharch). These charges are the jeweller's fee for crafting the piece and can add 8% to 20% on top of the gold price. Understanding how making charges work helps you budget correctly and negotiate better.

What Are Gold Making Charges?

Making charges are the labour and craftsmanship fee that a jeweller adds when selling handmade or machine-crafted gold jewellery. They cover:

  • Skilled artisan labour (goldsmithing, stone-setting, polishing)
  • Workshop overhead costs
  • Equipment and tools
  • Design fees for custom pieces
  • Shop overhead and profit margin

Unlike the gold content of a piece — which is refundable at the FENEGOSIDA rate when you resell — making charges are not recoverable at resale. When you sell gold jewellery back to a dealer, they pay only for the raw gold value, not the craftsmanship.

How to Calculate the Total Price of Gold Jewellery

The total price you pay for gold jewellery in Nepal is calculated as:

Total Price = Gold Value + Making Charges + VAT (13%)

Where: Gold Value = Weight in tola × FENEGOSIDA rate for the karat

Worked Example — 2 Tola 22K Necklace

FENEGOSIDA 22K rate: NPR 2,58,592 / tola

Weight: 2 tola

Gold value: 2 × 2,58,592 = NPR 5,17,184

Making charges at 12%: 5,17,184 × 0.12 = NPR 62,062

Subtotal: NPR 5,79,246

VAT at 13%: NPR 75,302

Total you pay: NPR 6,54,548

This means for a 2 tola 22K necklace with 12% making charges, you pay about NPR 1,37,364 more than the raw gold value. That extra amount is completely lost when you resell.

5 Tips to Pay Less in Making Charges

  1. Buy machine-made jewellery — machine-made chains and bangles have lower making charges (5–8%) versus handmade pieces
  2. Shop at Indrachowk or wholesale markets — making charges are usually lower in the main gold market versus mall jewellery stores
  3. Buy plain designs — the more intricate the design, the higher the making charge; simple pieces cost less to craft
  4. Negotiate on large purchases — for bridal sets or bulk purchases, dealers will often reduce making charges by 1–3%
  5. Avoid festival rush periods — during Dashain and Tihar, dealers have less incentive to negotiate; buy in off-peak months (January–March, June–July)

Are Making Charges Refunded When Selling?

No. When you sell gold jewellery in Nepal, dealers pay only the FENEGOSIDA buying rate for the weight and purity of the gold. The making charges you originally paid are not refunded. This is why gold jewellery is considered a lower-return investment compared to gold coins or bars, where the only premium is a small minting cost of 0.5–2%.

If you are buying gold purely for investment, gold coins are more cost-effective than jewellery because you lose significantly less on buying costs.

VAT on Gold Jewellery in Nepal

Gold jewellery in Nepal attracts 13% VAT on the total price (gold value + making charges). This is a government tax and cannot be avoided at licensed dealers. Some smaller shops may not charge VAT — this is technically illegal and means they may not be licensed, which creates risk when reselling. Always buy from licensed FENEGOSIDA dealers and ask for a VAT receipt.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is making charge fixed or negotiable in Nepal?

Making charges are not regulated by FENEGOSIDA — each shop sets its own rate. They are negotiable, especially for large purchases or repeat customers. Always ask for the making charge rate before placing an order.

Do I pay making charges on old gold exchange?

If you bring old gold jewellery to exchange for a new piece, most dealers will charge making charges on the new piece but deduct the gold value of what you bring in. The making charges on your old piece are not refunded.

What is the difference between making charges and wastage?

Some dealers quote making charges separately from "wastage" — a small deduction (0.5–2%) for gold lost during the crafting process. Watch out for dealers who charge both separately as this can significantly increase the total cost. Ask for a single all-in price.
