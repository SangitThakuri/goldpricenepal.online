If you've ever visited a gold shop in Nepal or checked the daily gold price, you've seen "NPR X per tola." But what exactly is a tola? How much does it weigh? And why does Nepal use this unit when the rest of the world uses grams and troy ounces? Here's everything you need to know.

What Is a Tola?

A tola (तोला) is a traditional South Asian unit of weight that has been used in the Indian subcontinent for centuries. It was originally the weight of a British-era silver rupee coin. The word comes from the Sanskrit "tula" (तुला), meaning balance or scale.

1 tola = exactly 11.6638 grams (commonly rounded to 11.664g)

Today, the tola remains the standard unit of gold measurement in Nepal, India, Bangladesh, and Pakistan, even as other commodities have switched to metric units.

How the Tola Is Used in Practice

When you buy gold in Nepal, weight is measured in tola, aana, and lal:

  • Large purchases (investment gold, heavy jewellery): quoted in full tolas (e.g., "5 tola necklace")
  • Medium jewellery (bangles, earrings): quoted in tola and aana (e.g., "2 tola 4 aana")
  • Small items (rings, small pendants): quoted in aana and lal (e.g., "8 aana 2 lal")

Why Nepal Uses Tola Instead of Grams

The tola system is deeply embedded in South Asian trade history. Despite metrification in most industries, the gold trade has maintained tola because:

  • All traditional price lists and dealer networks are built around tola
  • Consumers understand it — "5 tola bangles" is intuitive
  • Traditional weighing scales (takaas) are calibrated in tola
  • FENEGOSIDA officially announces rates per tola

India also uses tola widely in its gold trade, and since Nepal's market is closely linked to India's, the unit persists naturally.
