Is Gold a Good Investment in Nepal? Honest Analysis

An honest analysis of gold as an investment in Nepal — actual returns, import duty impact, comparison with FD and real estate, liquidity, and key risks.

Investment
📅 July 20, 2026 ⏱ 7 min read ✍️ goldpricenepal.online

Gold has long been Nepal's most beloved store of value — embedded in culture, tradition, and financial planning. But is buying gold actually a good investment by the numbers? Here's an honest, data-driven answer.

The Case FOR Gold Investment in Nepal

1. Strong Historical Returns

Based on FENEGOSIDA data, Nepal's 24K gold price rose from approximately NPR 1,78,000 per tola in June 2024 to NPR 2,82,100 in July 2026 — a gain of about 58% over two years, or roughly 29% annualised. This significantly outpaced bank FD rates (7–9%) and inflation (around 5–7%) over the same period.

2. Inflation and Currency Hedge

Nepal's Rupee has historically depreciated against the USD. Since gold is priced in dollars, a weakening Rupee automatically increases gold's value in NPR terms — even if international gold prices are flat. Gold thus acts as a natural hedge against currency risk.

3. High Liquidity

Unlike real estate, gold can be sold quickly at virtually any gold shop in Nepal. There's no registration, no waiting period, and no negotiation on fundamentals — the FENEGOSIDA rate is the price. You can convert gold to cash within hours.

4. No Income Tax on Capital Gains (Currently)

Nepal does not currently impose capital gains tax on profits from selling physical gold. This makes the net return from gold more favourable compared to, say, stock market gains which are taxed.

5. Cultural Acceptance and Collateral Value

Gold is universally accepted across Nepal — in cities and villages alike. It can be used as collateral for loans at banks (gold loan at 7–12% interest), and every family member understands its value.

The Case AGAINST Gold Investment in Nepal

1. The Import Duty Penalty (The Biggest Issue)

Nepal charges approximately 10% customs duty plus other levies on gold imports, meaning the domestic price is roughly 23–25% above the international spot price. This premium is your immediate loss the moment you buy.

⚠️ Example: International gold = NPR 2,24,000 equivalent. Nepal retail price = NPR 2,82,100. That's a ~26% premium built in. For you to profit, gold must rise more than 26% from international price just to break even on the premium paid.

2. Making Charges Are Non-Recoverable

If you buy jewellery, the making charges (8–18% of gold value) are entirely lost when you sell. A dealer buying back jewellery pays only the gold value, not the making charges. For investment, always buy plain bars or coins with minimal making charges.

3. Storage and Security Risk

Physical gold needs secure storage. Home storage risks theft. Bank locker fees add ongoing costs (NPR 3,000–8,000 per year). Unlike stocks or FDs, gold requires physical security.

4. No Passive Income

Gold pays no dividends, interest, or rental income. Unlike FDs (earning 7–9% annually) or rental property, gold only returns money when you sell it. The opportunity cost of capital locked in gold can be significant.

Best Approach: Gold as Part of a Portfolio

Financial advisors generally recommend allocating 10–20% of a portfolio to gold. In Nepal's context — where currency risk is real and banking options are limited — a slightly higher allocation (15–25%) may be justified.

Recommendations by goal:

  • Emergency wealth store: Keep 1–2 tola in physical gold
  • Medium-term investment (3–7 years): Gold can be a core holding
  • Short-term (under 2 years): Risky due to import premium — avoid
  • Regular saving: Consider buying small amounts monthly to average your entry price
